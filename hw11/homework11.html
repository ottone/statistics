Stochastic Process with Continuous Time and countinuous state (Brownian Motion)

This page provides an interactive simulation of a stochastic process with continuous time and continuous state, specifically a numerical approximation of a Brownian motion (also known as a Wiener process) defined on the interval [0, 1].

A Brownian motion is a fundamental model in probability theory, physics and financial mathematics. It describes the random evolution of a continuous variable over time and is characterized by:

  • continuous trajectories (no jumps),
  • independent increments,
  • normally distributed increments,
  • variance proportional to elapsed time.

In this simulation, the time interval [0,1] is divided into n equally spaced steps of length \( \Delta t = 1/n \).

The evolution of the process is driven by small random fluctuations applied at each time step. At every step, the current value is updated by adding a normally distributed random variation whose magnitude depends on both the time discretization and the volatility parameter σ. This mechanism can be summarized by the relation:

\( X_{t+\Delta t} = X_t + \sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}\,Z \)


where Z is a continuous random variable centered at zero, responsible for the random fluctuation at each step. σ represents the volatility (intensity of randomness) of the process.

The standard normal random variable \( Z \) is generated using the Box–Muller transform, a classical algorithm that converts two independent uniform random variables \( U_1, U_2 \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(0,1) \) into a Gaussian variable via:
\( Z = \sqrt{-2 \ln(U_1)} \cos(2\pi U_2) \)
This guarantees that the increments of the process follow a true normal distribution, which is a defining property of Brownian motion.

When multiple sample paths are simulated, the resulting trajectories exhibit the typical features of Brownian motion: irregular but continuous fluctuations, absence of directional bias, and statistical self-similarity across different time scales.

Furthermore, the distribution of the final value \( X(1) \) converges to a Gaussian distribution:
\( X(1) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \)
illustrating the fundamental connection between Brownian motion and the normal law.

This simulator allows you to explore how the choice of the volatility parameter σ and the discretization level n influence the qualitative and quantitative behaviour of the stochastic trajectories.

How to Use the Simulator

Attempts — Select how many independent realizations of the process you want to simulate. Each attempt represents one possible trajectory of the Brownian motion. Increasing this number allows you to better visualize the variability and statistical structure of the process.

σ (Sigma) — Controls the volatility of the process, that is, the intensity of its random fluctuations. A higher value of σ produces wider and more erratic trajectories, while a smaller value results in smoother and more concentrated paths.

n (Number of Steps) — Determines the temporal resolution of the simulation. The interval [0, 1] is divided into n smaller subintervals. Larger values of n make the simulated paths appear more continuous and closer to an ideal Brownian motion.

After selecting the parameters, the simulator automatically generates and displays:

  • multiple sample paths of the Brownian motion over time;
  • the distribution of the final values at time 1.

By adjusting σ and the number of steps, you can observe how randomness evolves over time and how the continuous fluctuations shape the overall behaviour of the process. The final values follow an approximately Gaussian distribution, illustrating one of the fundamental properties of Brownian motion.
