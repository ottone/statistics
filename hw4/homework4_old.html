Law of Large Numbers (LLN)

Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers states that as the number of independent, identically distributed trials increases, the observed frequencies and sample averages stabilize around their true (theoretical) values.

Frequency Convergence

If fn is the observed frequency of an event after n trials and p is the true probability, then:

\( f_n \longrightarrow p \quad \text{as } n \to \infty \)

Empirical Mean vs. Theoretical Mean

Let \(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n\) be i.i.d. random variables with expected value \(E[X_i] = \mu\). The empirical mean is:

\( \mu_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \)

\( \mu_n \longrightarrow \mu \)

Why Frequency Is a Mean

Define \(X_i = 1\) if the event occurs in trial i, and \(X_i = 0\) otherwise. Then the frequency is just the average of these 0–1 outcomes:

\( f_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i = \mu_n \)
