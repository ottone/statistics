Discussion: Analogies, Mathematical Properties, and Relationships

This assignment (Homework 7) extends the previous simulation (Homework 4), based on the Bernoulli process and the Law of Large Numbers (LLN), applying it to a new context ‚Äî a sequence of hacker attacks on a server. In both cases, each experiment (or attack) can result in one of two outcomes: success (+1) or failure (-1). Each trajectory therefore represents a discrete random walk on the integer line ‚Ñ§, where the cumulative score evolves as a sum of Bernoulli trials.

Just like in the LLN experiment, the underlying process here is a repetition of binary random events with probability p. As the number of trials (n) increases, the distribution of total outcomes approaches the binomial distribution. While the LLN focuses on the mean convergence of relative frequencies toward p, this task emphasizes the distribution of cumulative outcomes, showing how each trajectory contributes to the final histogram of ‚Äúsecurity outcomes‚Äù.

The probability that a trajectory ends at a specific final score y after n trials is given by: 

P(Y = y) = C(n, k) ¬∑ pk ¬∑ (1 - p)n - k, where  k = (n + y) / 2

Here, C(n, k) represents the binomial coefficient ‚Äî the number of distinct ways to obtain exactly k successes in n independent attempts. The histogram in the ‚ÄúSecurity Outcome‚Äù chart empirically counts how many simulated trajectories reach each final score, while the red binomial curve represents the theoretical distribution predicted by this formula.

Pascal‚Äôs Triangle (known in Italy as Tartaglia‚Äôs Triangle) provides a visual representation of all binomial coefficients. Each number C(n, k) in the triangle corresponds to a coefficient in the binomial expansion of (a + b)n. In this simulation, each layer of the triangle can be seen as the possible score distribution after n attacks ‚Äî showing how the number of combinations leading to each score grows symmetrically around zero. Highlighted entries correspond to scores actually observed in the simulation.

üåÄ Link with Binomial Expansion and Fibonacci Sequence

The binomial coefficients also appear in many combinatorial and numerical structures. The coefficients in successive diagonals of Tartaglia‚Äôs Triangle generate the Fibonacci sequence: 

Fn = C(n-1, 0) + C(n-2, 1) + C(n-3, 2) + ...

This connection reveals how binomial coefficients encode the recursive growth patterns of both probabilistic outcomes and natural sequences such as Fibonacci numbers.
